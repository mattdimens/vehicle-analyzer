Get wheel offset wrong, and you're looking at rubbing tires, stressed suspension components, or wheels that simply won't clear your brakes. It's one of the most commonly overlooked fitment specs, and one of the most consequential.

## What Is Wheel Offset

Wheel offset is the distance in millimeters between a wheel's hub mounting surface and its centerline. This measurement determines how far your wheel sits inward or outward in the wheel well. Positive offset tucks the wheel closer to the suspension, while negative offset pushes it outward toward the fender.

You'll find offset stamped on most wheels as "ET" followed by a number. ET40, for instance, indicates a +40mm positive offset. The "ET" comes from the German word "Einpresstiefe," which translates to insertion depth.

Three parts define offset:

- **Centerline:** The exact middle of the wheel's width, measured between the two bead seats where the tire mounts
- **Mounting surface:** The flat area on the back of the wheel that contacts your brake rotor and hub assembly
- **ET marking:** The stamped number showing offset in millimeters, found on the inner barrel or behind a spoke

Once you know where to look, reading offset becomes straightforward.

## Three Types of Wheel Offset

Offset falls into three categories based on where the mounting surface sits relative to the centerline. Each type positions the wheel differently in the wheel well.

### Positive Offset

Positive offset means the mounting surface sits closer to the street-facing side of the wheel. This pulls the wheel inward toward the suspension and strut assembly. Most factory vehicles run positive offset, typically between +35mm and +50mm on sedans and crossovers.

The result is a "tucked" look where the wheel sits well inside the fender. Front-wheel-drive cars almost always use positive offset because it keeps the wheel clear of steering and suspension components.

### Zero Offset

Zero offset places the mounting surface exactly at the wheel's centerline. This configuration is uncommon on production vehicles, though it serves as the reference point for positive and negative values. When you see ET0 stamped on a wheel, the mounting surface sits dead center.

### Negative Offset

Negative offset positions the mounting surface toward the brake side of the wheel, which pushes the wheel outward past the fender line. Lifted trucks, off-road builds, and classic muscle cars often run negative offset to create a wider, more aggressive stance.

Deep-dish wheels typically feature negative offset. The mounting surface sits so far back that it creates the concave appearance many enthusiasts look for.

## Wheel Offset vs. Backspacing

Offset and backspacing both describe wheel positioning, yet they measure from different reference points. Offset calculates distance from the centerline to the mounting surface. Backspacing measures from the mounting surface to the wheel's inner lip.

Here's where the distinction matters: two wheels with identical offset can have different backspacing if their widths differ. A wider wheel with the same offset will have less backspacing, which could cause the inner lip to contact suspension components or brake calipers.

When comparing wheels, checking both measurements prevents clearance problems. Offset tells you about positioning, while backspacing tells you about inner clearance.

## How to Find the Offset on Your Wheels

Before pulling out a tape measure, check whether the offset is already marked or documented. Most wheels have this information available if you know where to look.

### Check the Back of the Wheel for the ET Stamp

Look along the inner barrel or behind a spoke for a stamped marking like ET35 or ET45. The number after "ET" is your offset in millimeters. Some aftermarket wheels place this on a sticker near the valve stem hole instead of stamping it into the metal.

Paint, powder coating, or general wear can obscure stamps on older wheels. If you can't find a marking, the wheel may have been refinished or the stamp may be hidden under grime.

### Look Up Your OEM Wheel Specs Online

Your vehicle's owner's manual typically lists factory wheel specifications, including offset. Manufacturer websites and OEM parts databases provide the same information when you enter your year, make, and model.

This approach works well when replacing wheels with stock-equivalent sizes. Factory specs give you a reliable baseline for what fits your vehicle without modification.

## How to Measure Wheel Offset

When the ET stamp is missing or unreadable, you can calculate offset with a straight edge, a ruler or tape measure, and a flat surface.

### 1. Lay the Wheel Face Down on a Flat Surface

Remove the wheel from the vehicle and place it face-down on a level garage floor or workbench. The tire can stay mounted, though removing it makes measurement slightly easier.

### 2. Measure the Total Wheel Width

Place a straight edge across the back of the wheel, resting on both inner lips. Measure from the floor up to the bottom of the straight edge. This gives you the total width from bead seat to bead seat, not the outer lip edges.

### 3. Calculate the Centerline

Divide your total width by two. A wheel measuring 200mm wide has its centerline at 100mm from either edge. Write this number down for the final calculation.

### 4. Measure from the Mounting Surface to the Back Edge

Measure from the flat hub-mounting pad down to the floor. This tells you where the mounting surface sits relative to the wheel's inner edge.

### 5. Determine Your Offset Value

Subtract your centerline distance from the mounting surface measurement. A positive result means positive offset. A negative result means negative offset. Zero means the mounting surface sits exactly at centerline.

## What Happens When Wheel Offset Is Wrong

Incorrect offset creates fitment problems ranging from cosmetic issues to safety concerns. The consequences depend on whether the offset is too positive or too negative for your specific vehicle.

### Problems from Too Much Positive Offset

When offset is too positive, the wheel tucks too far inward. The tire may rub against inner fender liners, suspension arms, or brake calipers during turns or when hitting bumps.

Constant rubbing damages tire sidewalls and can lead to blowouts. Steering response often feels vague because the altered geometry affects how forces transfer through the suspension.

### Problems from Too Much Negative Offset

Excessive negative offset pushes wheels outward past the fender line. This creates stress on wheel bearings and suspension components that weren't designed for the altered load distribution.

Common symptoms include:

- **Steering wheel kickback:** The changed scrub radius causes the steering to react more aggressively to road imperfections
- **Accelerated bearing wear:** Wheel bearings handle loads at angles they weren't engineered for
- **Legal issues:** Tires extending past fenders violate vehicle codes in many jurisdictions

The altered geometry also changes how your steering feels and responds, which some drivers find unsettling.

## How to Choose the Right Wheel Offset

Selecting the correct offset involves balancing several factors beyond matching your current specs.

Factory offset is your starting point. Staying within plus or minus 5mm of OEM offset usually maintains proper clearance and handling characteristics. Beyond that range, you need to account for additional variables.

If you're changing wheel width, the extra width splits evenly inside and outside the original wheel position. A wider wheel at the same offset pushes both inward and outward, potentially causing rubbing in both directions. Adjusting offset compensates for this.

Suspension modifications change the equation too. Lifted or lowered vehicles often need different offsets because the suspension geometry has shifted. What cleared fine at stock ride height may rub after a 3-inch lift.

Your desired stance also drives the decision. Flush fitment, tucked wheels, or aggressive poke each require different offset ranges. And offset doesn't work in isolation. Bolt pattern, diameter, and tire size all interact with it, so one incompatible spec can make the whole setup fail.
